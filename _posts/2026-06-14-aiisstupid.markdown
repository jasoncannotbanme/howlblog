## Metacognition and Dunning Kruger Effect
Let's get down to something interesting first.
Metacognition and Dunning Kruger Effect go hand in hand. For the normies or people who have a life and don't psychoanalyze everything. Metacognition can be defined as the capacity to be aware of, monitor, and control your own mental processes, including how you learn, make decisions, and solve problems. And DKE can be defined as a cognitive bias where people with limited knowledge or competence in a specific area greatly overestimate their own skills.

Good, now that's out of the way. Here's the interesting thing.
Psychological research on metacognition while using AI and in general has shown that people typically estimate themselves better than they are also called the <span style="background-color: #dff800"> better than average effect</span> [(Brown, 1986)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illusory_superiority). A [2025 study in Computers in Human Behavior](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0747563225002262) found that while AI use improved task performance, participants simultaneously overestimated their own performance and those with higher AI literacy were actually *less* accurate at judging themselves. The more you know how to use it, the more you think you don't need to know anything else. People using AI also tend to trust its output over professionals who have spent years in the field. Just think about it how many times have you put your medical reports or income statements into a chatbot to explain it to you in an easier way instead of consulting an actual professional.
Low performers face DKE. This is solely because they don't optimise their interactions with AI which leads to poorer metacognitive monitoring.
However, people who know where they stand on the topic they are studying and using AI for, properly adjust the way they use AI to study that topic. They fill the gaps in their study by asking AI questions that they missed while studying that subject.

**The difference between how subjects in the study actually use AI is one of the most important factors in determining whether the subjects are facing DKE or not.**

## Self assessment
The heading pretty much describes itself, but looking into this further, Let's break down what self assessment is.
Self assessment is a subpart of what meta cognition is in this context. It describes a person's ability to, get this, *"Think about their thinking"*. It measures how effectively an individual plans their learning, monitors their understanding in real time and adjusts/evaluates their strategies when they face a problem in the subject they are studying.
Multiple studies have shown that people display low self-assessment when using AI to help with academic work with findings from [cognitive science](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0747563225002262), [education research](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11016976/), and [learning outcome analysis](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.16019v1) all pointing the same way. This is purely because while using AI, people are led to believe that they are simplifying the way they are studying. They do a few questions and truly believe they have grasped the core logic behind the topic they are studying without referencing the books out of pure laziness.
However, research has also shown that people who have a core grasp of the topic by reading their academic textbooks and class notes, and are using AI to fill in what they missed, display high self-assessment leading to better understanding and less dependence on LLMs. A [2024 mixed-methods study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11016976/) on AI-powered metacognitive self-regulated learning found high acceptance and positive outcomes specifically among students who used AI intentionally to close knowledge gaps rather than replace thinking entirely. Additionally, over-reliance on AI can make students less inclined to seek information independently and engage deeply with the material.

## How you use Generative Models
AI models have accumulated large amounts of training data since late 2022. Generative models like OpenAI's ChatGPT and Anthropic's Claude are trained on [datasets ranging from 45 terabytes to over a petabyte of data](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11472406/). How well these models respond depends on how structured and concise your prompting is and how you handle context windows inside the models you are using. Studies have shown that if generative models are not used correctly (ie: weak prompts and poor context window management) it can lead to weaker and non-significant outcomes. To counter this, major universities have now started to acquire subscriptions under enterprise/edtech plans and distribute them among students while also strengthening how students use generative models by teaching them the proper way of doing things.

## Privacy
If this hasn't been mentioned enough by thousands of security researchers, overreliance on AI models has created a privacy shithole between its users and the companies that host the AI models. There are risks associated with exposing personal information to unauthorized parties, raising privacy and security issues [(Wu et al., 2024)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2949715923000707). While companies like Anthropic and OpenAI have worked on maintaining a divide between the users, their chats and the data they are providing to the models for context and reasoning, they are prone to security flaws because models use the conversations of their users to train themselves unless you specifically deny access through user settings which most users don't (ie data scraping). This alone creates quite a havoc. Secondly, users frequently paste sensitive intellectual property, personal health records, or private contact details into AI chatbots. By default, consumer-facing AI tools often retain this data to train future models, potentially causing accidental data leaks where the AI unintentionally exposes the information in future outputs [F5](https://www.f5.com/company/blog/top-ai-and-data-privacy-concerns). To make it worse, generative models use a predictive interface. In layman's terms, even when you specifically make sure your data is anonymized, AI models are incredibly adept at inferring highly sensitive information such as political views, health statuses even to the extent of studying your browsing habits and thinking patterns unknowingly [transcend.io](https://transcend.io/blog/ai-and-privacy). All of this is practically the tip of the iceberg, which really puts this into perspective as to how insane the mass surveillance of its users is.

## What to take from all this
So, has AI made us stupid or faster? Honestly, both and neither, depending entirely on how you use it, I know its not the answer people are looking for.

Right now, most AI models are still in the developing phase. With every release, models are getting smarter and handling resource-intensive tasks at lower costs, which significantly reduces the input and increases the output, making it worthwhile for its users  but it comes with its pros and cons. People need a thorough understanding of how AI works, how to use AI itself (ie prompting, context handling) and its effects when using it for everything.

Using AI to fill in the grunt work that's slowing you down is smart. Using AI to autocomplete everything is where the problems begin. If you're a low performer who doesn't understand the subject matter and uses AI to substitute thinking entirely, you get dumber with DKE in full effect, overconfident and under-skilled. If you're someone who already knows their stuff and uses AI to close the gaps, you get faster.

The uncomfortable truth is that AI mirrors whatever habits and intentions you bring to it. It quite literally acts like a mirror of yourself, If you dont know what youre doing and use generative models with minimal context, you are blind siding yourself.

However there are smarter and more efficient ways of using AI. Students in general need to be taught by actual professionals on how to use generative models to help with studying as LLM's on a large scale store enough context to help students study their subject more effectively and effciently if correctly used.
